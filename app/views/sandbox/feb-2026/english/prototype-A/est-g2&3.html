You must pay Statutory Sick Pay (SSP) if your employee is eligible.

Check your employee’s eligibility and calculate how much you need to pay (opens in a new tab). You pay SSP instead of a normal salary. You cannot reclaim this cost from the government.

Paying extra sick pay

Some employers choose to pay their own sick pay. This is in addition to SSP. If you offer additional sick pay you can decide when to start and stop paying it, and how much to pay.

Asking for medical evidence

You should not ask your employee to provide proof of their health condition or disability from a medical professional.

After your employee has been off work for 8 days or more you can choose to ask for medical evidence.

! Warning You must pay Statutory Sick Pay (SSP) if your employee is eligible.

Check your employee’s eligibility and calculate how much you need to pay (opens in a new tab). You pay SSP instead of a normal salary. You cannot reclaim this cost from the government.

Paying extra sick pay

Some employers choose to pay their own sick pay. This is in addition to SSP. If you offer additional sick pay you can decide when to start and stop paying it, and how much to pay.

Asking for medical evidence

You can choose to ask for medical evidence of your employee’s absence before you pay SSP. Examples include a letter from a hospital, or a fit note (opens in new tab). Fit notes used to be called sick notes.

Keeping records of conversations and meetings

If you do ask for medical evidence, remember to keep a record of when you asked and what you received. This could help to protect your business if you need to show evidence of your communication with your employee.

If your employee is off work for more than 28 weeks

SSP stops being paid to employees after 28 weeks. Some employers choose to continue to pay their own sick pay after this time.
