Decide on changes to support your employee

Make any changes you can to help your employee. They can often be temporary and more affordable than you might expect. If your employee is disabled, some changes might be legally required.

The changes you make will depend on your employee’s needs. For example, you could provide:

  • a closer car parking space for someone who struggles to walk longer distances
  • more breaks for someone with fatigue
  • reduced public-facing tasks, if that environment makes someone’s mental health worse

As well as changing your employee’s role or work environment, you can change the way they work. For example, some people prefer to do different tasks or fewer hours when their condition is worse.

See more examples of changes on the Equality and Human Rights Commission website (opens in a new tab)

Avoiding discrimination against disabled employees

By law you must make reasonable adjustments if you could be expected to know that your employee is disabled. If you are not sure, you might want to make changes for any employee with a health condition.

If you do not make reasonable adjustments for a disabled employee you would be discriminating against them. Your employee could make a claim to an employment tribunal.

What is ‘reasonable’ will depend on your business. If an employee uses a wheelchair and you do not have space for a lift, you would not need to change premises. But you would need to consider other ways for them to be able to do their job, such as working from somewhere else.

Help with costs from Access to Work

If adjustments are more expensive than is reasonable for you, your employee can apply to Access to Work (opens in a new tab) to help with the costs.
